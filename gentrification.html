Gentrification and Social Mixing: Uncovering Patterns of Social Mixing

About the Project

Anyone living in Hyde Park, the neighborhood surrounding University of Chicago’s campus, has a front row seat in witnessing gentrification in Chicago. However, gentrification is not just a phenomenon tied to UChicago’s seat in the South Side, and is experienced across large swathes of the city, according to the Urban Displacement Project. Historically, the story of gentrification is patched together using census data, giving a low granularity view on the impacts of individuals’ lives who are affected by gentrification. However, through the use of anonymized cell phone data, we were able to piece together a better understanding of the social dynamics influenced by gentrification in Chicago.

The novelty of this data necessitated a novel way of viewing it. I was brought onto this project as the web designer and developer, tasked with creating a website which would help paint the story of gentrification through another lens.

Learning about the Problem

What does experienced exposure mean? How is that different from residential exposure? What do these mean in the context of gentrification?

To gain a better understanding of gentrification and its impact, I conducted research by drawing upon my knowledge from various courses at the University of Chicago and immersing myself in relevant literature and geography-based readings. Although I was not directly involved in data collection or cleaning, this research served as a foundation for grasping the complexities of the issue and designing a better website.

Additionally, I looked through existing visualizations to gain inspiration and see what else is out there. When conducting this exploration, I imagined myself to be our target user: someone in academia, perhaps at a school like UChicago. They could be a student or professor looking to learn more about the impact of gentrification in Chicago and have seen this website presented at a symposium, since that’s what the postdoc I was working with had envisioned as the purpose of the website. However, we didn’t want to limit the audience just to people in the field of academia. With gentrification being a hot-button issue, we wanted to build a site that was approachable to the general public—a tool that they could use to explore the impacts of gentrification in their own city. Through these two lenses, I evaluated numerous other websites to gain a better understanding of how they conveyed their data set in a legible, engaging, and accessible way.

Design Process

Ideation

Wenfei, the primary researcher, and I sat down and brainstormed a myriad of possible features from interactive graph components to text based stories. Based on my technical knowledge and experience, I was able to do some research into each of these features and estimate a time cost—given our five month timeframe, we sought to design a site that satisfied our main problem while remaining possible to complete.

Concurrently with the ideation process, each of us asked people in our circles to describe how they would want to interact with a data set such as the one we had. Both Wenfei and I had access to different audiences— Wenfei worked with many other researchers at the Mansueto Institute for Urban Innovation, while I talked to several students who had never thought critically about a map before. After modifying our ideas with some preliminary feedback from our target audience, I began the next phase of the design process.

Sketching and Wireframing

Based on the initial exploration, we opted for a simple sitemap—just the map page and an about page. From a style standpoint, we knew we were going to use the Fomantic UI development framework to style the website, so we decided to keep the presets. Most styling which would shape a user’s interaction happened in the map layer; keeping a simple, clean UI for the rest of the site would place emphasis on the visualization itself.

The majority of the wireframing centered around the way the map would display on a page and where the user’s controls would be. With this, we played with a few layouts but ultimately opted for a full-width, full-height map with semi-opaque control panels over-laying the top. Based on my research, this seems like a common layout to maximize the size of the map. It’s worth noting here that we designed the website exclusively for desktop use.

Prototyping

With our wireframe designed, I proceeded to prototyping the final layout. Because of the simplicity of the layout, the prototyping mostly served as a way to think through layer hierarchy (what layers appear first? How do you toggle through other layers?). We used these prototypes to collect another round of informal feedback from peers and friends, leading to a few conclusions moving forward:

1. The response to our storytelling feature was very positive. People liked the guided interaction through the website because it made the data less daunting to digest.

2. It’s not enough just to have the three types of exposures being toggled. How can we better contextualize this within other data from the city?

3. The initial base map color scheme (light grey and dark grey) is confusing because the water is darker than the land. People did like the simplicity because it wouldn’t detract from the information.

Implementation

As the designer and developer, I had the opportunity to execute our vision and bring the design to life. Over four months I built the website, which can be found here, with Mapbox GL JS and Fomantic UI. The site is currently hosted on Github Pages. I had some basic HTML and CSS experience preceding this project, but hadn't worked extensively with Javascript, nor had I built a website from start to finish. Working on this project not only enabled me to refine my UX skills, but also further solidified my understanding of the interplay between design and implementation.

Impact

I presented Visualizing Gentrification in Chicago: Uncovering Patterns of Social Mixing at the Spring Undergraduate Research Symposium at the University of Chicago. This event provided valuable opportunities to showcase the visualization, engage with the audience, and gather feedback. The insights gathered from these interactions were incorporated into my subsequent thesis project, Visualizing Chicago Rideshare Costs.
